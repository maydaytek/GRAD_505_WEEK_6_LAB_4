The Consumption file (Consumption.xlsx) contains data on the eating habits of individuals along with information on their sex, mass, and age. This data file was obtained from a translated national food consumption data survey conducted by the Institute of Nutrition, Mahidol University, Thailand. The data was collected between March 2014 and April 2015 with three rounds to cover seasonal variation of food consumption. Participants came from 16 provinces from Thailand and the Bangkok metropolitan.

The Pesticide Residual file (Pesticide Residue.xlsx) has data on the amount of pesticides that were found on cabbage and Chinese kale that were bought from a market and then tested. The residue data comes from two studies that are also included in this lab module for reference (Wanwimolruk et al. 2015, Wanwimolruk et al. 2017).

The provided table LD50.xlsx lists LD50 values (Lethal Dose 50%) for five pesticides, which indicate their acute toxicity (short-term poisoning potential).

What is LD50?

Definition: LD50 is the amount of a substance required to kill 50% of a test population (usually rodents like rats or mice) under controlled conditions. It is a standard measure of acute toxicity, expressed in milligrams of pesticide per kilogram of body weight (mg/kg).

How to Interpret LD50 Values?

  • Lower LD50 values = Higher toxicity (e.g., Malathion: 0.0003 mg/kg is extremely toxic).
  • Higher LD50 values = Lower toxicity (e.g., Dichlorvos: 0.0339 mg/kg is less toxic than Malathion but still highly dangerous)

Introduction

During this lab, we will merge these three datasets into a single document to perform future analysis. Before beginning the lab, start thinking about the questions we may want to answer.

For example, one question might be: "Is there a group (e.g., Age, Sex, Sex_Age_class) at a higher risk to pesticide exposure? Does this depend on the type of vegetable?"

A testable hypothesis for this question could be: "The youngest ages are at the highest risk to pesticide exposure."

First, risk can be quantified as the sum of the products of individual exposures and toxicities:

R i s k = ∑ k = 1 n e x p o s u r e k × t o x i c i t y k {"version":"1.1","math":"$$Risk = \sum_{k=1}^n exposure_k \times toxicity_k $$"}where n here is the number of pesticides exposed to.

This equation is a specific example of a very general equation in risk analysis and cost-benefit analysis. The next time you are faced with making a decision based upon the potential positive and negative effects of the choices you make, you could employ this. This would simply be the sum of the products of the likelihood of an effect happening and the "goodness" (positive) or "badness" (negative) of those outcomes should they happen.

The exposure of each test subject (our study replicates are individual people) will depend upon how much pesticide remains on different foods and how much of that food they eat:

e x p o s u r e = ∑ 1 f ∑ 1 p f o o d f × p e s t i c i d e s p {"version":"1.1","math":"$$exposure = \sum_{1}^f \sum_{1}^p food_f \times pesticides_p $$"}This equation simply means there may be many foods eaten and each food may have many different pesticides on it.

Open the Consumption.xlsx file. This gives the amount (mass) of cabbage and Chinese Kale eaten by each subject (so f=2) within a certain period.

The residue data (Pesticide Residue.xlsx) has information on the amount of each type of pesticide on cabbage and Chinese Kale. Therefore, we will simply combine the data from these two files to be able to calculate the total exposure of each test subject.

One very important consideration is units. A numeral without a unit is almost always a useless datum. The metadata (embedded in the table or listed as a README sheet in the Consumption.xlsx case) should include information on the units for all variables. It may be helpful to include units in the variable names where a given unit is not very standard (e.g., pH). Never vary the units within a single variable/column. We need to ensure before calculating exposure that the amount of food and the amount of pesticide are using the same units.

Examine the metadata or README to ensure that the units match. We will need to get these values into the same units before taking their product. But first, we need to calculate how much is eaten over a given period. The frequency column in the consumption data has units of month-1. We will calculate the risk on a monthly basis. Because the data on the amount eaten of each type of produce is given in grams, we will begin with grams and then convert as necessary. Begin by making a new column that is the product between frequency and consumption. Name this column "Amount_Eaten".

We will use the Lethal Dose 50 LD50 (same units as pesticide residuals) values of the different organophosphate-based pesticides as a measure of their relative toxicities. LD50 is a standard measure used in toxicology to indicate the dose of a substance that is required to kill 50% of a test population. Because we must sum across these in the risk calculation, having these values in the equation puts the resulting exposure values on the same scale.

  9. Relative exposure - Compare to standard

    The exposure must be compared to a known standard in order to calculate the relative effect by age, sex, or class. 

    The reference dose for Disulfoton is 0.0025 mg/kg/day based on U.S. EPA’s guidelines. The reference dose is an estimate (with uncertainty spanning perhaps an order of magnitude) of a daily oral exposure to the human population (including sensitive subgroups) that is likely to be without an appreciable risk of deleterious effects during a lifetime. 

    We will compare the calculated exposure to the reference dose of Disulfoton to convert each exposure value to a percentage value indicating the relative exporsure. For example, if the exposure you calculated is 0.00018mg/kg/day for your target population group, then you will divide the exposure by 0.0025mg/kg/day, which is 7.2%. That means the cumulative exposure of this pesticide group did not exceed the reference dose, and it is unlikely to produce the adverse effect to this target population group.
